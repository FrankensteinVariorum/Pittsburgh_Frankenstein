The first nations whose names are on record are Chaldea
and India. Tradition has preserved wonderful accounts of their
progress in astronomical science. To them we owe the Zodiac
and many observations on the motions of the heavenly bodies
which argue a much more accurate knowledge of that art
than any succeeding country could boast uptil the time of Galileo
and Newton. But their wisdom passed away from the earth leaving faint
traces among the Eyptians a race of men whose
minds bear a considerable affinity with those of the
Chinese. Both these people arrived at a certain point in
refinement and knowledge and remained fixed: Superstiti
on and slavery clouded their perceptions and a hatred of
innovation, the never-failing attendant on a mind
which has learnt every thing from imitation and no-
thing from itself, was the natural consequence of the
arbitrary government they obeyed. Their history both on ac-
count of its antiquity and the genius of the nation presents
little more than a list of names. Pride and a love of Con
quest was the distinguishing characteristic of their kinds
kings and their magnificance was displayed either by a bloody
and useless warfare on the liberties of their neighbours
or by the oppression and destruction of their own
subjects employed in the building of those famous pyra
mids, which, being scarcely more than shapless masses
of brick or stone, seem fitted to endure till the earth
itself shall be no longer.


The oppression of their own sovereigns fitted
the necks of theEgyptians for the yoke of a foreign prince
after a few generations they were subdued and remained for
ages swallowed up in the great Persian monarchy till after the
death of Alexander when they again became
anindependant nation till the overpowering
force of the Roman Empire reduced them once more to the
condition of a Province.
