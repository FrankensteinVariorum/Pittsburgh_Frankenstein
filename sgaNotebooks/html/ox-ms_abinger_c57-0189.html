The memory of Cyrus is contemplated with peculiar
complacency by most historians. His humanity and love
of justice distinguishes him those other conquerors to whom
ambition is the ruling motive. Cyrus was educated in the
strictest simplicity, early enured to fatigue and taught
after the manners of his age, to consider glory as the great &
desirable bourne towards which every virtuous man ought
to direct his exertions But the natural mildness of his
disposition let him the rather to seek for honour in the
don than in the punishment of his enemies; and his
often appeared to the fallen nation rather in the light of
blessing than a curse. Innumerable anecdotes are
of his virtue and wisdom by Xenephon but this history,
tunately must be regarded as a romance rather than the real
biography of the man: for although tradition might probably
preserve several of the private actions of so
celebrated a prince. Yet the minuteness & regularity of detail
which embellishes the narratives of the Cyropedia must
be solely attributed to the invention of its author and there
are no rational grounds by which we can regulate the ex
tent of credit due to the historian of Cyrus. The anecdotes
however that are perserved of him chiefly relate to the generous
pardon of his prisoners of war, a mode of proceeding that
wonderfully facillitated his conquests so that he was still young
when he became possessed of the Babylonish Empire. From
this time he was principally occupied in the cares of govern
ment which he established on a very arbitrary footing
leaving to his sucessors a despotic authority over a people
